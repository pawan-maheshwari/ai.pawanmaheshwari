Why 90% of Claude Code Users Are Missing Its Most Powerful Feature

By Pawan Maheshwari — AI Transformation Lead · TCS

Every Claude Code user knows the feeling. You type a big task — refactor the auth layer, plan a multi-service migration, redesign the database schema — and then your terminal goes quiet. The cursor blinks. You wait. You can't type. You can't work. You're essentially a hostage in your own workflow while Claude thinks.

"Anthropic's bet is that planning — not code generation — is the highest-leverage moment in AI-assisted development. Ultraplan is their answer."

The Problem Ultraplan Solves

Standard Claude Code planning runs entirely in your local terminal session. Every token generated for the plan consumes context. Every second Claude spends thinking, your terminal is blocked. For a two-file fix, this is a minor inconvenience. For a thirty-file architectural refactor, it's a workflow killer.

Worse, the terminal's linear review interface means you're either accepting, rejecting, or typing vague follow-ups. There's no way to annotate a specific section of the plan without re-prompting the entire conversation.

What breaks with standard planning:

  • Terminal freezes for the full duration of planning — no parallel work
  • Planning consumes your main session's context window
  • No inline annotation or targeted revision — just accept/reject
  • Longer tasks risk hitting token limits mid-plan
  • No way to review the plan in a structured browser interface

What Ultraplan Actually Does

Ultraplan offloads the entire planning phase to Anthropic's cloud infrastructure. When you invoke it, Claude Code spins up a remote session running Opus 4.6 in Anthropic's Cloud Container Runtime — with up to 30 minutes of deep, uninterrupted thinking time. Your local terminal polls every 3 seconds for status updates, but remains completely free for other work.

The plan materialises in a dedicated browser interface where you can leave inline comments on specific passages, request targeted revisions, and react with emoji. When you're satisfied, you click "Approve" — and the plan is teleported back to your local terminal, ready for execution.

Three Ways to Invoke Ultraplan

  • Direct Command: Type /ultraplan followed by your task description.
  • Keyword Detection: Include the word "ultraplan" anywhere in a natural prompt.
  • From Plan Mode (Recommended): Run a local plan first with Shift+Tab, then choose "Refine with Ultraplan" in the approval dialog.

Real-World: Migrating Auth from Sessions to JWTs

Scenario: Enterprise Node.js API — 18 services, live production traffic. Your team needs to migrate a monolithic Express.js authentication layer from server-side sessions to stateless JWTs — touching 18 microservices, a shared Redis session store, 4 mobile clients, and live production users.

A standard local plan would take 8+ minutes and block your terminal. With Ultraplan: the cloud session runs for ~12 minutes, delivers a 7-section architectural plan, accepts one inline comment ("Need shadow mode for 48h overlap"), revises Section 4, and teleports the approved plan back — with your terminal free the entire time.

"The signal to use Ultraplan is when you're asking for less than you actually need — because you're afraid of freezing your terminal."

Requirements & Availability

  • Claude Code v2.1.91 or later (auto-provisioned from v2.1.101)
  • Claude Code on the web account (claude.ai/code)
  • A connected GitHub repository for cloud execution
  • Not available on Amazon Bedrock, Google Cloud Vertex AI, or Microsoft Foundry

Ultraplan is currently in research preview — fully functional for accounts that meet the requirements above. The infrastructure for choosing planning depth is already built. Treat it as an advanced tool for your most complex, highest-stakes work.

The Judgment Imperative: Five Principles for AI Oversight That Scales

By Pawan Maheshwari — TCS AI Transformation · Governance & Strategy · 15 min read

We are living through the most consequential organizational transformation since electrification. AI systems now write code, triage medical images, approve loans, draft contracts, and summarize board meetings. And yet something uncomfortable persists at every boardroom conversation: who, exactly, is accountable for what these systems do?

The honest answer, in most enterprises today, is no one — or more precisely, everyone assumes someone else is watching. The result is a fragile architecture of assumption layered over automation, held together by the thinnest possible membrane of human review.

Principle 01 / 05
Verify Designs, Not Just Outputs

Most organizations treat human oversight as a checkpoint — something that happens after an AI system has already produced a result. The real leverage of human judgment lies not in reviewing outputs but in shaping the architecture of systems before they produce anything. Who decides the confidence threshold? Who designs the test suite? Who decides which contexts should not rely on AI at all? A single senior data scientist embedded at the design stage would have caught a systematic flaw in week one.

Principle 02 / 05
Don't Rely on Human Verification Alone

The same systems that create the need for verification also make comprehensive human verification impossible. An AI that processes a million transactions a day cannot be meaningfully overseen by a team checking every output. The math doesn't work. Build AI-human workflows where the AI handles volume and the human handles variance. A major insurer restructured its claims AI — 94% automated, 6% to experienced adjusters. Claim volume 20× what it was. Human review hours stayed flat. Quality improved.

Principle 03 / 05
Invest in Human Expertise

There is a seductive efficiency logic: if AI can do this task better and faster, why maintain the human capability? The answer is strategic: human expertise is the substrate on which effective AI oversight depends. If you hollow it out, you lose the ability to govern the systems you've built. Efficiency gains forgone to preserve human expertise should be treated as strategic investments — not inefficiencies.

Principle 04 / 05
Verify What Is Learned, Not Just What Is Produced

Organizations are meticulous about verifying AI outputs — and almost completely incurious about verifying the conclusions they draw from those outputs. When a pilot returns positive results, what exactly was measured? A retail chain measured a 7% margin increase in 50 stores — and approved national rollout. The analysis missed that the gain was concentrated in stores with strong inventory management. National rollout produced a 2% margin decline.

Principle 05 / 05
Treat Verification as a Strategic Imperative

Build structured after-action reviews into every AI deployment cycle. Convene independent reviewers who were not part of the pilot team. Explicitly ask: what result would we have needed to see to conclude this didn't work? The organizations that will govern AI well are those that treat verification not as overhead, but as the strategic capability that protects everything else.

7 Critical Skills to Build Production-Ready AI Agents

By Pawan Maheshwari · TCS

The role of an AI professional is shifting from simple prompt engineering to full-scale agent engineering. To ship robust, production-grade agents, engineers must master seven core disciplines.

01
System Design — Foundation

Structured architectures where LLMs, databases, and tools work together reliably. Sketch your agent's full data flow before writing code. Study LangGraph or AutoGen for battle-tested patterns. Each component should be independently swappable — loose coupling is the foundation.

Tool & Contract Design — Contracts

Strict schemas for tool inputs to prevent the LLM from making dangerous hallucinations. Use Pydantic for every tool input. Ambiguous schemas are the primary source of production hallucinations. A validated lat/lon coordinate is valid; a city name string is not.

SchemasPydanticTool APIs
03
Retrieval Engineering — Context

Effective RAG via smart chunking, embeddings, and re-ranking for high-quality context. Try LlamaIndex chunking + Cohere Rerank. Measure retrieval precision, not just output quality.

RAGEmbeddingsRe-ranking
04
Reliability Engineering — Resilience

Manage external API failures with retry logic, back-off strategies, timeouts, and circuit breakers. Wrap every external call in exponential back-off + circuit breaker. Define fallback paths before launch — never a dead end.

Retry LogicCircuit BreakerBackoff
05
Security & Safety — Safety

Protect against prompt injections, validate inputs, and enforce permission boundaries. Red-team your own agent — attempt prompt injection attacks on yourself. Add Guardrails AI or LLM Guard before exposing users. Location fuzzing, injection filters, read-only token scopes.

Prompt GuardValidationPermissions
06
Evaluation & Observability — Metrics

Tracing and data-driven testing to debug failures — move from vibes to measurable performance. Add LangSmith or Langfuse tracing immediately. Maintain eval datasets and run regression tests on every model change.

TracingLLM EvalsLangSmith
07
Product Thinking — Experience

Design for the human experience — clear expectations, graceful error handling, and knowing when to escalate to a human. Write an "agent spec" defining escalation triggers, uncertainty communication, and edge-case behaviours before deployment. Agents without graceful failure UX erode user trust — and trust, once lost, is hard to recover.

The Rich Get Richer — Even in AI: The Matthew Effect in Academic Research Tools

Adapted from Jefferson Pooley's essay "The Matthew Effect in AI Summary," LSE Impact Blog, May 2026. Expanded and humanised for general readership by Pawan Maheshwari.

Imagine you're a junior researcher in Lagos, Nairobi, or Lima — brilliant, rigorous, publishing in peer-reviewed journals. Then a colleague in Boston opens an AI research tool, types your exact topic, and the tool returns a neat summary built almost entirely from scholars at Oxford, Harvard, and MIT. Your work doesn't appear. It might as well not exist.

This is not a hypothetical. It is, increasingly, the lived reality of what happens when we hand the task of synthesising knowledge over to AI — and it has a name borrowed from the Bible.

What Is the Matthew Effect?

In 1968, Columbia sociologist Robert Merton coined the Matthew Effect, borrowing from the Gospel of Matthew: "For to every one who has will more be given, and he will have abundance; but from him who has not, even what he has will be taken away."

In plain terms: famous scholars get more famous. Their papers get cited more, which makes them appear more authoritative, which leads to still more citations. The less-cited scholar drifts toward obscurity — not necessarily because their work is worse, but because it never entered the visibility loop in the first place.

Visibility doesn't just reflect quality. It creates it. The more a paper is cited, the more credible it appears — and the more it will be cited again.

The Matilda Effect — A Bias With a Name

In 1993, historian of science Margaret Rossiter named a parallel phenomenon the Matilda Effect — after Matilda Gage, a nineteenth-century suffragist who documented how women's intellectual contributions were systematically attributed to their male colleagues. The Matthew Effect doesn't just disadvantage isolated individuals. It amplifies patterns already inscribed in the system — shaped by colonialism, patriarchy, and the dominance of English-language publishing in the Global North.

Enter the AI Research Tool

Over the past eighteen months, Google, OpenAI, Anthropic, Microsoft, and Perplexity all launched "deep research" tools with near-identical promises: ask a question, receive a researched summary with citations. Academic publishers followed — Elsevier, Clarivate, and Digital Science all released AI-powered literature assistants.

The tools are impressive. But underneath the polished output lies a structural problem: when we ask an AI to summarise "the literature," the tool doesn't read papers the way a human does — it operates by predicting, statistically, which content is most likely relevant based on patterns learned from a vast training corpus. That corpus is the accumulated record of published scholarship — including all of its biases.

"The models may act as laundering machines — context-erasing abstractions that disguise their probabilistic reasoning and give old inequalities a fresh coat of algorithmic legitimacy." — Jefferson Pooley, LSE Impact Blog

The Privilege Multiplier

The old Matthew Effect was traceable. You could follow citation trails and document patterns of exclusion. Scholars could investigate and push back. The new AI-mediated version is different: it operates inside a black box. Early empirical evidence confirms this worry — AI research tools systematically surface papers from high-prestige institutions and English-language publications at rates disproportionate even to those papers' citation counts. The tools are acting as "privilege multipliers."

Consider a researcher in Brazil studying urban poverty. The global academic literature is stratified: US and UK journals receive more citations, are included in more databases, and carry more prestige metrics. An AI tool trained on this literature will weight those sources more heavily. The Brazilian researcher who has documented ground-level realities in a Portuguese-language regional journal may simply be invisible to the tool — not because their work is less rigorous, but because the system never valued it equally.

What This Means — and What Comes Next

For individual researchers: treat AI summaries as a starting point, not a verdict. Seek literature databases that prioritise inclusivity over citation prestige. Make a habit of searching for voices from outside the dominant publishing centres.

For institutions and funders: the AI research tools being deployed at scale are not neutral. Demand transparency from AI tool providers, invest in diverse citation databases, and recognise that a tool being widely used does not make it equitable.

For AI developers: invest in bias audits, diversify training data, and create mechanisms for users to surface under-represented perspectives. These are not optional extras — they are the price of deploying these tools responsibly in high-stakes epistemic environments.

"The researcher in Lagos who didn't make the summary didn't fail to deserve inclusion. The algorithm simply never learned to see her."

Analysis · AI & Society · June 2026
The Institutions AI Will Need — and the Ones We Must Invent

Based on Daniel Björkegren's "The Institutions AI Will Need: Notes from Class," Columbia SIPA, May 2026. Humanised and expanded by Pawan Maheshwari.

Somewhere in a seminar room at Columbia's School of International and Public Affairs, a group of public administration students — not engineers, not Silicon Valley insiders — are doing something quietly radical. They are deciding what AI-transformed society should look like. And that, it turns out, may be exactly the right group to ask.

"AI is rewriting the rules of society. The question is not whether institutions will change — but whether we will shape that change, or merely survive it." — Björkegren's course framing, Our AI Future, Columbia SIPA

Why Institutions, Not Just Intelligence

Most discourse treats AI as something to be bolted onto existing institutions — a new clause added to the employment contract, a new module in the compliance training. Daniel Björkegren's course asks a harder question: What if the institutions themselves need to be rebuilt?

Consider an analogy: when electricity arrived, the initial instinct was to wire existing factories for electric light. It took decades to realize that electricity didn't just illuminate existing factories — it allowed factories to be redesigned entirely. The productivity gains were delayed not by technology, but by imagination. AI is likely no different.

Five Provocations That Cut to the Bone

Provocation 01
Is the Potato a General Purpose Technology?

Research found that the introduction of the potato from the Americas accounted for roughly a quarter of Old World population growth between 1700 and 1900. Not a processor — a tuber. Does a technology need to be "general purpose" to be historically transformative? And just because we call AI a GPT, does that guarantee it transforms everything? Labels can mislead. Evidence, less so.

Provocation 02
Should Self-Driving Cars Respect Local Values?

The Moral Machine experiment showed striking differences in how people worldwide prioritise lives in unavoidable accidents. Nigerian respondents showed greater willingness to spare higher-status individuals. Chinese respondents were less inclined to prioritise the young. Americans were more willing to sacrifice human lives to spare animals. Who sets the ethical parameters of AI systems deployed globally?

Provocation 03
Today's AI Policies Will Look Quaint

In the early days of aviation, the US built giant concrete arrows — some stretching 70 feet across — that pilots could follow from the cockpit. Within two decades, obsolete. The AI governance frameworks being debated today — disclosure requirements, watermarking, model registries — are the concrete arrows of our era. Not wrong; contextually appropriate stopgaps. But the landscape will shift faster than the regulations.

Provocation 04
Visualising What We Cannot Intuitively Grasp

The scale of modern AI systems is genuinely incomprehensible to the unaided imagination. It is very difficult to regulate — or even meaningfully discuss — a system you cannot visualise. One of the underrated institutional needs of the AI era is better tools for public understanding — not dumbed-down, but genuinely illuminating.

Provocation 05
The AGI Timeline Question Is a Policy Question

If AGI arrives in five years, the institutional responses needed are radically different from a twenty-year horizon. Policy timelines, democratic deliberation cycles, and investment horizons all depend on the answer — and no one knows with confidence.

The Institutions We Actually Need

  • A New Epistemology for Policy: Academic rigor produces carefully measured estimates — typically three to five years out of date by the time they influence policy. AI is moving faster than the academic production cycle. New institutions for "rapid evidence synthesis" — honest about uncertainty, useful at speed — are urgently needed.
  • Grading AI Against AI: If AI can produce a competent policy memo for $0.01, the value of a human doing the same drops toward zero — unless the human does something the AI cannot. Institutions that train knowledge workers will need to explicitly define what that something is.
  • Democratic Processes for Algorithmic Values: The institution for deciding AI values does not yet exist at the scale needed. Market choice is insufficient. Regulatory fiat is blunt. Genuinely deliberative processes — closer to citizens' assemblies — may be part of the answer.
  • Legibility Infrastructure: A society cannot govern what it cannot understand. Auditors, journalists, judges, and voters all need tools to make AI systems legible. Almost none of them have it today.

What the Students Built

By year two of Björkegren's course, every student — including those with no prior coding experience — built a functioning software demo as their final project. The demos addressed real institutional gaps: an AI early-warning system for policy turbulence, a career exposure assessment tool for AI displacement, a plain-language legal rights explainer, and a nuanced content filter that returns agency to the individual.

None required a computer science degree. All address institutional failures that existed long before AI, and that AI is now making tractable.

"The most important AI institution may not be a regulator or a standards body. It may be a new kind of translator — between the technical and the civic, between what AI can do and what society actually wants."
